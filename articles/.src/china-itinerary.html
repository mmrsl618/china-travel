China Itinerary: Beijing, Xi'an, and Shanghai in 14 Days

Last updated: June 2026.

Two weeks is enough time to see China's three most iconic cities without rushing. Beijing gives you imperial history and the Great Wall. Xi'an delivers the Terracotta Warriors and ancient city walls. Shanghai wraps things up with skyline views, excellent food, and easy day-trip options. This guide lays out a realistic 14-day plan with 2026 entry rules, train booking logistics, and the advance-ticket strategy that makes or breaks the trip.

Before You Go: Entry Rules and the Booking Strategy

As of June 2026, passport holders from 50 countries — including the UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Japan, South Korea, Brazil, and most EU member states — can enter China visa-free for up to 30 days. This policy runs through December 31, 2026. Travelers from countries not on the visa-free list (including the United States) need an L visa. Check the National Immigration Administration website for the current list before booking anything.

Every traveler arriving in China must also complete the China Digital Arrival Card (CDAC). Submit it at s.nia.gov.cn within 72 hours before your flight lands. You will need your passport details, flight number, seat assignment, and the address of your first hotel in China. The form takes about ten minutes. Save the confirmation QR code — you will scan it at the immigration counter on arrival.

The real bottleneck on this itinerary is tickets. The Forbidden City releases tickets exactly seven days before your visit date at 8:00 PM Beijing time, and peak-season slots can sell out in under five minutes. Set a calendar reminder and be on the booking page a few minutes early. The Terracotta Warriors site requires advance booking as well, though tickets are easier to secure — book through Trip.com, Klook, or the official WeChat mini-program one to two weeks ahead. High-speed train tickets open 15 days before departure on the 12306 app or Trip.com. Book your long-distance segments the moment the window opens, especially if traveling during a Chinese holiday.

Best time to go: April through May and September through October. These months give you mild weather across all three cities. Avoid the first week of October (National Day) and Chinese New Year (late January or February), when domestic travel surges and everything from train tickets to museum entry becomes extremely difficult to secure.

Beijing: Days 1 to 5

Land in Beijing and give yourself a relaxed first day. Take the Airport Express (25 CNY, about 25 minutes to Dongzhimen) or a DiDi ride into the city. Settle into your hotel, walk around the neighborhood, and get your bearings. If you have energy in the evening, head to Wangfujing or Nanluoguxiang for your first street-food stroll.

On day two, visit the Forbidden City. Book your ticket through the official Palace Museum WeChat mini-program — it now accepts foreign passports and email registration. Entry costs 60 CNY in peak season (April to October) and 40 CNY off-peak. Add the Treasure Gallery and Clock Gallery for 10 CNY each. The site is closed every Monday, so plan around that. Give yourself at least four hours inside. Enter through the Meridian Gate at the south end and exit through the Imperial Garden at the north. From there, cross the street to Jingshan Park (2 CNY) and climb the hill for the best overhead view of the entire palace complex.

Day three belongs to the Great Wall. Mutianyu is the best section for a first visit — well-restored, less crowded than Badaling, and equipped with a cable car and a toboggan slide for the descent. The round trip from central Beijing takes a full day. Book a driver through your hotel or use DiDi (expect around 500–700 CNY round-trip). The wall ticket costs 45 CNY; the cable car is 140 CNY round-trip. Go early — leaving by 7:30 AM puts you on the wall before the tour buses arrive.

Days four and five cover the rest of Beijing's core sights. Visit the Temple of Heaven in the morning (entry 15 CNY, through-ticket 34 CNY including the main halls), then walk through the nearby hutong neighborhoods. The Summer Palace (30 CNY peak season) deserves a half-day on its own — take metro Line 4 to Beigongmen and walk the lakeside长廊 (Long Corridor). In the evenings, try Peking duck at a restaurant like Siji Minfu or Dadong, and explore the hutongs around Gulou for drinks in converted courtyard bars.

Beijing to Xi'an by High-Speed Train

The train from Beijing West Station to Xi'an North Station covers roughly 1,200 kilometers in about four and a half hours. There are around 20 G-class high-speed departures daily between 7:00 AM and 6:00 PM. A second-class seat costs 515.5 CNY, first class is 824.5 CNY, and business class runs 1,627.5 CNY. Second class is comfortable — the seats recline, each row has a power outlet, and the ride is smooth enough to work or nap.

Book tickets through the 12306 app or Trip.com. Both accept foreign passports. Trip.com adds a small service fee but offers an English interface and accepts international credit cards, which makes it the easier choice for most visitors. Tickets open 15 days before departure, and morning departures on Fridays and Sundays fill up first. Each adult passenger can bring 20 kg of luggage (maximum 130 cm total linear dimensions), and there is a small rack at the end of each carriage for larger suitcases. Arrive at the station 45–60 minutes before departure — you will need your passport for the security check and ticket gate.

Xi'an: Days 6 to 8

Xi'an was the capital of thirteen Chinese dynasties and the eastern terminus of the Silk Road. Today it is a lively mid-size city with some of China's best street food and the most intact ancient city wall in the country.

On day six, after the morning train arrives, drop your bags and head to the Xi'an City Wall. Entry is 54 CNY. Rent a bike on top of the wall (45 CNY for 90 minutes) and ride the full 13.7-kilometer loop — it takes about an hour at a relaxed pace and gives you a unique view of the city's old and new neighborhoods. In the evening, walk to the Muslim Quarter near the Drum Tower for dinner. Try lamb skewers (羊肉串), roujiamo (肉夹馍, a meat-stuffed flatbread), and cold noodles (凉皮).

Day seven is for the Terracotta Warriors. The site is about 40 kilometers east of central Xi'an. Take Tourist Bus 5 (also called Bus 306) from Xi'an Railway Station — the ride costs 7 CNY and takes roughly one hour. A DiDi ride takes about 45 minutes and costs 100–150 CNY. The ticket is 120 CNY, bookable through Trip.com or Klook. Inside, Pit 1 is the main attraction — thousands of life-sized soldiers standing in battle formation. Pit 2 and Pit 3 are smaller but worth walking through. Plan on two and a half to three hours at the site, then take the bus back to the city.

On day eight, visit the Shaanxi History Museum. Entry is free, but you must reserve a time slot in advance through the museum's WeChat account; tickets are released daily and go fast. The collection spans from the Zhou dynasty through the Tang, with some of the finest bronze vessels and Tang-era gold work anywhere in China. Spend the afternoon at the Big Wild Goose Pagoda (entry 50 CNY, pagoda climb 30 CNY extra), then find a spot for Xi'an's famous yangrou paomo (羊肉泡馍, lamb soup with torn flatbread) before your evening departure.

Shanghai: Days 9 to 14

The high-speed train from Xi'an to Shanghai takes six to eight hours (second class around 670 CNY). Alternatively, a flight takes about two and a half hours and can cost as little as 500 CNY when booked in advance. Decide based on your preference: the train is more scenic and avoids airport transfers at both ends, while a flight buys you half a day in Shanghai.

Shanghai is the most international of the three cities, and you will feel it immediately. Start with the Bund on your first evening — the colonial-era buildings on one side, the Pudong skyline across the river on the other. Walk from the Waibaidu Bridge south to the Yan'an Road intersection; the full stretch takes about 30 minutes without stops. For the classic skyline photo, go around sunset when the buildings begin to light up.

On day ten, go up the Shanghai Tower (180 CNY). At 632 meters it is China's tallest building, and the observation deck on the 118th floor gives you a panorama that stretches for kilometers on a clear day. Book online to skip the ticket queue. Afterward, walk to Yu Garden (30 CNY) and the surrounding Yuyuan Bazaar — it is touristy but worth seeing, especially the Ming-era rockeries and pavilions inside the garden itself.

Spend days eleven and twelve at a slower pace. The French Concession is Shanghai's most walkable district — tree-lined streets, 1920s lane houses, independent coffee shops, and small boutiques. Start at Fuxing Park and wander west toward Wukang Road and Anfu Road. The Shanghai Museum on People's Square is free and has an outstanding collection of ceramics, calligraphy, and bronze. In the evening, try xiaolongbao (soup dumplings) at a local restaurant — Jia Jia Tang Bao and Lin Long Fang are two reliable neighborhood spots that do not require reservations.

Days thirteen and fourteen are for day trips. Suzhou is 25 to 60 minutes away by high-speed train (second class around 40 CNY). Visit the Humble Administrator's Garden (80 CNY) and walk the canals along Pingjiang Road — Suzhou's old town feels like stepping into a classical Chinese painting. Alternatively, Hangzhou is about 50–90 minutes by train (second class around 75 CNY). Walk or bike around West Lake (free), stop at a lakeside tea house, and visit Lingyin Temple (75 CNY). Both cities are easy to reach and easy to navigate with a map app. Return to Shanghai in the evening for a final dinner — maybe on the Bund or in a rooftop bar in Jing'an — before your departure flight the next day.
