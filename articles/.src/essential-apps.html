Essential Apps for Traveling in China

Last updated: June 2026.

You have your visa, your flight is booked, and your suitcase is packed. Then you land in Beijing, pull out your phone, and realize: Google Maps shows you standing in a lake, you cannot hail a taxi on the street, and the restaurant menu is entirely in Chinese characters. The digital tools you rely on at home do not work here. China runs on a parallel app ecosystem, and walking into it unprepared turns simple tasks into genuine problems.

This article covers the core apps every traveler needs: the two super-apps that process payments and connect you to services, the map that actually knows where you are, the ride-hailing tool that works in English, the train-booking platform that accepts a foreign credit card, and the connectivity solution that keeps you in touch with the outside world. If you set these up before departure, your phone becomes a functional tool the moment you step off the plane instead of a glass brick.

One note before we begin: these apps cover the essential functions that touch nearly every moment of your trip. For a deeper walkthrough on the payment apps specifically — card linking, identity verification, spending limits, and troubleshooting — read our full Mobile Payment in China guide. What follows here assumes you will use that guide for the payment setup details and focuses on the app strategy as a whole.

Set up Alipay and WeChat before departure

Every essential-apps guide for China starts with the same two names, and for good reason: Alipay and WeChat are not optional. Together they handle mobile payment, and mobile payment covers more than 86% of retail transactions in China. From department stores to street-food carts, from museum ticket counters to subway gates, the expectation is that you scan a QR code to pay. If you arrive without at least one of these apps set up, you will struggle to buy a bottle of water.

For a detailed walkthrough on linking your foreign card, verifying your identity, and handling payment limits, read our full Mobile Payment in China guide. The short version is this: you can register Alipay using a foreign phone number, link a Visa or Mastercard directly to the wallet, and start paying within minutes of landing. WeChat Pay works the same way, with the added benefit that WeChat is also the primary messaging app in China — hotel staff, tour guides, and new friends will ask for your WeChat QR code instead of your phone number.

One crucial tip: complete identity verification in both apps before you travel. Alipay requires a passport photo and a selfie. The name you enter must match your passport exactly, including middle names. A mismatch here is the single most common reason verification fails. Without verified identity, your payment limits stay low and some features remain locked.

If you only install one app from this list, make it Alipay. It covers payment, ride-hailing, public transport codes, and bike-sharing — all inside a single app with an English-language interface.

Navigation: which map app actually works in China

Google Maps does not work in China. Not in the sense that it is blocked — it loads. The problem is deeper: China uses a different coordinate system called GCJ-02, which applies an offset to GPS data for national security reasons. All foreign map providers receive raw GPS coordinates and plot them on standard maps. The result is a consistent offset of anywhere from 50 to 500 meters. You will see your blue dot standing in the middle of a river when you are on a bridge, or floating inside a building when you are on the sidewalk outside.

If you have an iPhone, the solution is built in. Apple Maps licenses its China data from Amap (AutoNavi, also called Gaode Maps), one of the country's top mapping providers. When your phone detects that you are inside China, it automatically switches to local map data while keeping the entire interface in English. It shows accurate subway exits — a critical detail in cities like Shanghai where the wrong exit can add 15 minutes to your walk — bus route numbers, real-time traffic, and even indoor maps of large shopping malls.

If you use an Android phone, your best option is Amap (Gaode Ditu). The interface is almost entirely in Chinese, but the map itself is highly visual: subway lines are color-coded, exit numbers are clearly marked, and walking routes are easy to follow even without reading the labels. Baidu Maps is the alternative and offers a limited tourist-oriented mode with some English labels, but experienced travelers generally prefer Amap for its more accurate traffic data and pedestrian directions.

Before you leave home, download Apple Maps if you are on iPhone — it needs no extra setup. Android users should install Amap from their usual app store while still abroad, since Chinese app stores require a Chinese account. You will not be able to easily download it after arrival.

Getting a ride: use Didi inside Alipay, not the standalone app

Hailing a taxi by waving your hand on the street is essentially impossible in major Chinese cities. The entire ride-hailing market belongs to Didi Chuxing, which operates like Uber but with near-total market coverage. The key mistake travelers make is downloading the standalone Didi app from the App Store or Google Play.

The standalone Didi app is built for the Chinese market. Its interface is entirely in Chinese and it often requires a mainland Chinese phone number for full functionality. Fortunately, there is a far better approach: open Alipay, search for "DiDi" in the app's search bar, and use the Didi mini-program embedded inside Alipay.

This mini-program version gives you a fully English interface without any additional download or registration. When you enter a destination in English, the system automatically translates it to Chinese for the driver. When you pay at the end of the ride, the fare is charged to your linked Alipay card — no cash, no negotiation, no confusion. The Didi mini-program also includes a text-to-speech feature: type a message like "I am at the hotel lobby entrance" and the driver hears it in Mandarin.

You can also hail a Didi through the WeChat mini-program if you prefer to keep ride-hailing separate from payments. The experience is nearly identical. The critical principle is the same either way: do not download the standalone Didi app. Use the mini-program inside a super-app you already have.

Booking trains and long-distance travel

China's high-speed rail network is the largest in the world, and you will almost certainly use it for intercity travel — Beijing to Xi'an, Shanghai to Hangzhou, Chengdu to Chongqing. The official booking channel is a government app called 12306, named after the railway service hotline. In theory, it accepts foreign passport numbers for registration. In practice, the verification process is notoriously unreliable: it frequently rejects passport scans, the interface is entirely in Chinese, and customer support assumes you speak Mandarin.

The recommended alternative is Trip.com, the international-facing version of China's largest online travel agency. Trip.com lets you search train schedules in English, book with a foreign credit card, and receive automatic reissue notifications if the train schedule changes — a common occurrence on Chinese railways. The tradeoff is a small service fee, usually around 20 to 35 yuan (roughly USD 3 to 5) per ticket. For most travelers, the English interface and reliable booking process are well worth the cost.

For intra-city public transport, your best tool is once again Alipay. Open the app, tap "Transport," and you get a QR code that works on buses and metro systems in most Chinese cities. The code is city-specific — you may need to switch to the local transport card when you change cities — but the process takes seconds and the interface is in English. No separate transport card app is needed.

Staying online: eSIM beats VPN for daily use

China's internet firewall blocks access to Google services, Gmail, Instagram, WhatsApp, Facebook, and many Western news sites. If you want to stay in touch with people back home or use apps that depend on Google infrastructure, you need a way around these restrictions.

In previous years, the standard advice was to install a VPN before traveling. VPNs still work as a backup — particularly if you plan to use hotel Wi-Fi, which routes through the local censored internet — but they have become increasingly unreliable. During sensitive political periods, VPN connections are aggressively throttled or blocked entirely. Even when they work, running a VPN constantly drains your phone battery noticeably faster.

In 2026, the better solution for mobile data is an international eSIM. Providers like Nomad, Airalo, and Holafly sell China data plans that route your traffic through a foreign carrier, typically based in Hong Kong or Singapore. Because your phone is technically roaming on a non-Chinese network, the firewall does not apply. Instagram loads normally. Gmail works. Google Maps even loads — though as explained above, it is still inaccurate for navigation.

Set up your eSIM before departure. Most providers let you purchase a plan online, scan a QR code to install the eSIM profile, and activate it upon arrival. A 7-day plan with 10 GB of data typically costs USD 15 to 25, which is more than enough for maps, messaging, and occasional social media. Keep a VPN installed as a fallback for hotel Wi-Fi, but rely on the eSIM for your daily connectivity. The battery savings and reliability difference are significant.
