Beijing Travel Guide — Attractions, Food, and Getting Around

Last updated: June 2026.

Beijing rewards planning. It is enormous — sixteen times the area of London — and its famous sights are spread far apart. The Forbidden City alone contains over 900 buildings, and the nearest section of the Great Wall sits 70 kilometres outside the city centre. A good trip here is not about seeing everything. It is about picking the right attractions, knowing when to visit them, and understanding how the metro connects them.

This guide covers the four sights that define a first visit to Beijing, the best seasons to come, what to eat, which neighbourhoods make the best bases, and how to get around on the metro. For general guidance on using Chinese metro systems and ride-hailing, see our local transport guide — here we focus on what is specific to Beijing.

When to Visit Beijing

Beijing has four distinct seasons and the differences between them are stark.

Autumn (September to November) is by far the best time to visit. Daytime temperatures sit between 10°C and 25°C, the sky is often a clear deep blue — the famous "Beijing autumn sky" — and the humidity of summer has gone. The city's parks and the hills around the Great Wall turn gold and red. October, in particular, is close to perfect. The one week to avoid is the first week of October, which is Golden Week, a national holiday during which every major sight is packed and hotel prices double or triple.

Spring (April and May) is the second-best window. Temperatures are mild, trees and flowers are blooming, and the sandstorms that occasionally hit in March have usually passed by mid-April. The Labour Day holiday in early May brings another domestic travel surge, but it is shorter than Golden Week — typically three to five days — and slightly less intense.

Summer (June to August) is hot, humid, and crowded with domestic tourists on school holidays. Daytime highs regularly exceed 35°C, and July and August bring most of Beijing's annual rainfall, often as sudden heavy downpours. On the plus side, the long daylight hours mean sights stay open later, and the Summer Palace is genuinely pleasant in the early morning before the heat builds.

Winter (December to February) is cold — temperatures routinely drop below -10°C at night — but the skies are clear, the crowds are thin, and the Forbidden City under snow is spectacular. If you can handle the temperature, January and February offer the quietest experience of Beijing's sights. Avoid the two weeks around Chinese New Year (late January or February, depending on the lunar calendar), when much of the city shuts down and intercity transport is overwhelmed.

Must-See Attractions

Four sights define a first trip to Beijing. Each demands at least half a day, and they are spread across different parts of the city, so do not try to squeeze more than two major sights into one day.

The Forbidden City (Palace Museum) is the centrepiece. This was the imperial palace for 24 emperors across the Ming and Qing dynasties, and its scale is difficult to overstate: 980 buildings across 72 hectares. You enter through the Meridian Gate at the south end and exit through the Gate of Divine Might at the north. A one-way walk along the central axis, pausing at the three great halls and the imperial garden, takes two to three hours. Adding the side halls and the Treasure Gallery can stretch the visit to four or more.

Tickets must be booked online in advance — you cannot buy them at the gate. Reservations open seven days before your visit date at 8:00 PM Beijing time on the official Palace Museum website (intl.dpm.org.cn). You will need your passport number. Tickets for the peak season (April to October) cost 60 RMB; off-peak is 40 RMB. The museum is closed every Monday, except when a Monday falls within a national holiday week. Enter through the Meridian Gate, which you reach by taking metro Line 1 to Tian'anmen East or Tian'anmen West station and following the signs south of the square.

The Great Wall is not one wall but a network of sections, and choosing which one to visit shapes your entire day. Mutianyu is the best choice for most visitors: it is fully restored, less crowded than Badaling, and has a cable car and a toboggan slide. Badaling is the closest section to Beijing and the most famous — and also the most crowded, particularly in summer. Both are about 70 to 80 kilometres from the centre. A trip to either takes a full day. For Mutianyu, take metro Line 2 to Dongzhimen, walk to the Transport Hub, and catch bus 916 Express to Huairou, then transfer to a local shuttle or taxi for the final 20 kilometres. For Badaling, take the S2 suburban railway from Huangtudian station (connected to Line 8 and Line 13) or the high-speed train from Beijing North Railway Station. Entry costs 40 to 45 RMB, plus the cable car if you use it.

The Temple of Heaven is smaller and more manageable than the Forbidden City, and it rewards an early start. The park area opens at 6:00 AM, and the historic buildings — including the Hall of Prayer for Good Harvests, the iconic triple-eaved circular structure you see in every photograph — open at 8:00 AM. Arrive before 8:00 and you can walk the park grounds while local residents practise tai chi, sing, and play traditional instruments in the covered corridors. A through ticket covering the main buildings costs 34 RMB in peak season and 28 RMB off-peak. The easiest way to reach the Temple of Heaven is metro Line 5 to Tiantan Dongmen station, exit A or D, which puts you at the East Gate. Plan on 90 minutes to two hours.

The Summer Palace sits in the northwest of the city and works well as a half-day trip. The complex centres on Kunming Lake — most of the site is water — with the Long Corridor, a 728-metre covered walkway lined with painted scenes, running along the northern shore. The Marble Boat, the Seventeen-Arch Bridge, and the hilltop Tower of Buddhist Incense are the key sights. In summer, you can rent a boat on the lake. The grounds are large, so wear comfortable shoes. Entry costs 30 RMB in peak season and 20 RMB off-peak. Take metro Line 4 to Beigongmen station, exit D, to the North Palace Gate entrance. Allow three to four hours.

Getting Around Beijing

Beijing's metro is the most practical way to get around the city. The network has over 25 lines, and the four major attractions in this guide are all connected by it. If you have already set up Alipay's Transport QR code or a Beijing Yikatong transport card — both covered in our local transport guide — you can walk through the fare gates at any station after a quick security screening.

Here is how the metro connects to the sights covered in this guide:

Attraction Metro Line Station
Forbidden City Line 1 Tian'anmen East / West
Temple of Heaven Line 5 Tiantan Dongmen
Summer Palace Line 4 Beigongmen
Lama Temple Line 2 / Line 5 Yonghegong
Nanluoguxiang (hutongs) Line 6 / Line 8 Nanluoguxiang
798 Art District Line 14 Wangjing South

Trains run from roughly 5:00 AM to 11:00 PM, with last-train times posted at every station. A single ride costs 3 to 9 RMB depending on distance. Rush hours — roughly 7:30 to 9:00 AM and 5:30 to 7:30 PM on weekdays — are genuinely packed. Crowding on Line 1, Line 4, and Line 10 can be intense. If you are travelling between sights, aim for mid-morning or early afternoon when trains are less crowded.

For journeys where the metro is impractical — late at night, or with heavy luggage — DiDi, the Chinese ride-hailing app, is the most reliable option. You hail a car through the DiDi mini-program inside Alipay or WeChat, enter your destination in English, and the app handles translation for the driver. A taxi hailed from the street is also an option, though drivers rarely speak English, so have your destination written in Chinese characters or shown on a map. Fares are low by international standards: a cross-city trip of 15 kilometres typically costs 40 to 60 RMB.

Both of Beijing's airports connect to the metro. From Beijing Capital International Airport (PEK), the Airport Express train runs to Dongzhimen on Line 2 and Sanyuanqiao on Line 10. The ride takes about 20 minutes and costs 25 RMB. From Beijing Daxing International Airport (PKX), the Daxing Airport Express connects to Caoqiao on Line 10 in about 20 minutes for 35 RMB. Both express trains are separate from the regular metro and require a separate ticket or scan of your transport QR code at dedicated gates.

What to Eat in Beijing

Beijing's food culture is built around wheat rather than rice, and the city's signature dishes reflect the colder northern climate: hearty, savoury, and designed to keep you warm.

Peking duck (Beijing kaoya) is the dish the city is known for, and it is worth seeking out a proper restaurant for it. The duck is roasted until the skin is thin, crisp, and deep amber in colour. It is carved at your table and served with paper-thin pancakes, spring onion, cucumber, and sweet bean sauce. Wrap a slice of duck with the accompaniments in a pancake and eat it in one or two bites. Three restaurants are consistently recommended by locals and visitors alike: Siji Minfu (四季民福), which balances quality and price and has several branches across the city, including one with a Forbidden City view near the East Gate; Da Dong (大董), which is more upscale and experimenting with leaner, "super-lean" roast duck; and Quanjude (全聚德), the historic brand founded in 1864 — though opinions on its current quality are mixed. Expect to pay 200 to 400 RMB for a whole duck serving two to three people.

Zhajiangmian (炸酱面) is Beijing's everyday noodle dish: thick wheat noodles topped with a dark, savoury sauce made from fermented soybean paste and minced pork, surrounded by a ring of fresh shredded vegetables — cucumber, radish, soybean sprouts. You mix everything together at the table. It is a simple, satisfying meal that costs 15 to 30 RMB at most neighbourhood noodle shops. Look for small restaurants with "老北京炸酱面" (old Beijing zhajiangmian) on the sign.

Dumplings (jiaozi, 饺子) are not unique to Beijing, but the city has a strong dumpling culture, particularly in winter. A plate of boiled pork-and-chive or pork-and-cabbage dumplings, dipped in black vinegar with a touch of chilli oil, costs 20 to 40 RMB. Chain restaurants like Xian De Lou (馅老满) and countless small dumpling shops across the city serve them fresh.

For street food, the Huguosi Snack Street (护国寺小吃街) near Ping'anli station is a better bet than the more tourist-oriented Wangfujing Snack Street. Huguosi has been a food street for decades and the shops here serve authentic Beijing snacks: wandouhuang (pea flour cake), lüdagun (glutinous rice rolls with red bean paste), and aiwowo (sticky rice balls). You will also find Beijing yogurt (lao suannai, 老酸奶), a thick, mildly sweet fermented milk sold in ceramic jars at street stalls and convenience stores across the city — a refreshing break on a hot day and a genuinely local experience.

Where to Stay in Beijing

Beijing's hotel districts each have a distinct character, and your choice of neighbourhood will shape your daily experience as much as your choice of sights.

Dongcheng and Wangfujing are the most central options. Staying here puts you within walking distance of the Forbidden City, Tiananmen Square, and Wangfujing shopping street. Wangfujing has the highest concentration of international-brand hotels — the Peninsula, the Waldorf Astoria, the Crowne Plaza — as well as mid-range chains. The area is clean, well-lit, and easy to navigate, though it lacks the character of the older neighbourhoods. A short walk north into the Dengshikou and Dongsi areas puts you among quieter streets and smaller hotels with more local atmosphere while staying within a 15-minute walk of the metro and the Forbidden City. Expect 400 to 1,200 RMB per night for a comfortable mid-range room in this area.

Chaoyang is Beijing's modern centre: wide avenues, high-rise buildings, international restaurants, and the Sanlitun bar and shopping district. It is further from the historic sights — the Forbidden City is a 20- to 30-minute metro ride — but it is where you go for nightlife, dining variety, and the familiarity of a global city neighbourhood. The area around Liangmaqiao and Sanyuanqiao stations has several good hotels and is a 15-minute taxi ride from the airport express terminal.

Xicheng and the Houhai area offer lakeside streets, traditional courtyard houses, and a slower pace. The Houhai lakes are surrounded by bars and restaurants, and the nearby hutongs are among the best-preserved in the city. Several boutique hotels have converted traditional courtyard houses (siheyuan) into guest rooms — smaller and often pricier than chain hotels at 800 to 2,000 RMB per night — but they offer an experience a standard hotel cannot match. The area is well connected by metro Line 6 (Beihai North station) and Line 8 (Shichahai station).

If your trip involves onward high-speed train travel, consider staying near Beijing South Railway Station. This area is less scenic than Dongcheng or Xicheng, but it saves you a cross-city journey on departure day. From Beijing South, trains to Shanghai, Xi'an, and other major cities depart throughout the day, and the station connects to metro Line 4 and Line 14. Hotels here are generally mid-range business hotels in the 300 to 600 RMB range, with easy access to the metro for sightseeing.
