China 144-Hour Transit Policy

Home › Before You Go › Visa Policies

Last updated: June 2026.

You are planning a multi-stop trip across Asia — Tokyo to Bangkok, or Seoul to Singapore — with a layover in China. While searching flights, you notice that routing through Beijing or Shanghai is not only cheaper, but also gives you a chance to explore another city along the way. The catch: you do not have a Chinese visa. Can you still get in?

The answer is yes. This is China's 144-hour transit without visa policy — your confirmed onward ticket to a third country is itself your entry credential. Six days, zero cost, enough time to explore one of China's major cities. The problem is that many travelers get turned away at the airport, not for lack of a visa, but because their flight routing is wrong, they picked the wrong port, or their passport is not on the eligible list. Information circulating online is often outdated — the port list and eligibility rules change almost every year. This guide uses the latest policy to break down each step, so you know exactly what to check before booking your tickets and can avoid the common pitfalls.

What Is the 144-Hour Transit Without Visa Policy

The 144-hour transit without visa policy, formally the Transit Visa Exemption Policy administered by China's National Immigration Administration (NIA), is a unilateral entry facilitation arrangement. The core logic: as long as you hold a confirmed ticket from China to a third country or region, you can enter through designated ports without a visa and stay for up to 144 hours.

  • No advance application required. The permit is issued on the spot by border inspection at the port of entry. No embassy visit, no forms to fill in advance, no appointment, no fee. Bring your passport and your onward ticket — that is all.
  • This is not a visa. It is a completely separate channel from the L visa you would apply for at a Chinese embassy or consulate. There is no visa sticker involved. Border inspection stamps a temporary entry permit into your passport with the departure deadline clearly noted. The stamp becomes void once you exit China.
  • This is a policy, not a right. It is offered unilaterally by the Chinese government, which may adjust the eligible country list, port coverage, and permitted stay duration at any time. The rules you see when you depart may differ from those a month later — this is why this guide emphasizes checking the latest official announcements right before you travel.

What often causes confusion is that China actually has three tiers of transit without visa arrangements: a 24-hour version that applies at all open ports with no movement restrictions, a 72-hour version that has largely been phased out in favor of 144 hours, and the 144-hour version that offers the broadest coverage but comes with the tightest constraint — not the time limit, but the geographic activity zone, which is explained in detail below.

Eligibility Requirements

To use the 144-hour transit without visa policy, you must satisfy all five of the following conditions. Missing any one of them means the permit will not be issued.

  • 1. Your passport nationality is on the eligible list. The policy covers approximately 50-plus countries, including the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, Australia, New Zealand, Japan, South Korea, Germany, France, Italy, Spain, the Netherlands, Switzerland, Sweden, Norway, Denmark, Finland, Austria, Belgium, Portugal, Ireland, Poland, the Czech Republic, Hungary, Greece, Singapore, Malaysia, Thailand, Indonesia, the Philippines, Vietnam, as well as Russia, Brazil, Argentina, Mexico, and Chile. Before departure, check the latest list on the NIA's official website at www.nia.gov.cn. If your nationality is not on the list, the policy cannot be used regardless of how perfect your itinerary looks.
  • 2. You hold a connecting ticket to a third country or region. Your itinerary must follow the pattern: Country A → China → Country B, where A and B are not the same country. "Connecting ticket" means a confirmed, issued itinerary with reserved seats. Example: Tokyo to Shanghai to Singapore — eligible. London to Beijing and back to London — not eligible. However, Hong Kong to Guangzhou to Bangkok — eligible, because Hong Kong is treated as a third destination separate from mainland China for immigration purposes. San Francisco to Shanghai to Taipei — also eligible. Important: if you buy the two legs separately on different airlines and need to collect and re-check your luggage in China, border inspection may not recognize the journey as a coherent transit.
  • 3. Both your entry and exit ports are on the approved list and fall within the same policy region. A classic mistake: flying from Los Angeles into Shanghai, then flying out from Beijing to Tokyo. Shanghai belongs to the Shanghai-Jiangsu-Zhejiang region, while Beijing belongs to the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei region — crossing regions means border inspection will deny entry. The correct approach: enter through Shanghai and exit through Shanghai, or Hangzhou, or Nanjing — all within the same Shanghai-Jiangsu-Zhejiang region.
  • 4. You stay within the policy's permitted geographic zone. The Beijing ports allow movement within Beijing, Tianjin, and all of Hebei province. The Shanghai ports allow movement within Shanghai, Jiangsu, and Zhejiang. The Guangzhou ports are limited to Guangdong province only. The Shenyang and Dalian ports are limited to Liaoning province only. Other single-city ports are typically restricted to the city itself or its province. The regional boundary is a hard line — entering through Beijing and taking a high-speed train to Xi'an may only be four-plus hours by rail, but it is an illegal crossing. Border inspection can verify your actual movements at exit using hotel registration records and train ticket data.
  • 5. You pass border inspection at entry. The border inspection officer has final discretion. Even if you meet the first four conditions, you may still be refused if you cannot present a hotel booking, your itinerary appears suspicious, or you have a prior record of overstaying. It is advisable to carry: your original passport, a printed copy of your third-country flight itinerary, hotel reservation confirmations within the permitted zone, and a rough daily itinerary.

Eligible Cities and Ports

  • Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei region. After entry, you may move freely within Beijing, Tianjin, and all of Hebei province. Air ports: Beijing Capital International Airport, Beijing Daxing International Airport, Tianjin Binhai International Airport, Shijiazhuang Zhengding International Airport. Rail port: Beijing West Railway Station (for arrivals on international trains such as the K3 from Moscow). Sea port: Tianjin Port.
  • Shanghai-Jiangsu-Zhejiang region. The most heavily used region — Shanghai, Hangzhou, Suzhou, and Nanjing are all within this zone. Air ports: Shanghai Pudong, Shanghai Hongqiao, Hangzhou Xiaoshan, Nanjing Lukou. Sea ports: Shanghai Port, Ningbo-Zhoushan Port. The dense high-speed rail network means you can comfortably visit two or three cities within six days.
  • Guangdong region. Air ports: Guangzhou Baiyun, Shenzhen Bao'an, Jieyang Chaoshan. Movement is restricted to Guangdong province. Note: land entry from Hong Kong through Shenzhen checkpoints falls under a separate Hong Kong-Macau border transit policy and is not covered here.
  • Liaoning region. Air ports: Shenyang Taoxian, Dalian Zhoushuizi. Movement is restricted to Liaoning province.
  • Other participating cities. Xiamen, Qingdao, Wuhan, Chengdu, Kunming, Xi'an, Chongqing, Changsha, and Zhengzhou have also been added over time. Single-city ports typically restrict movement to the city itself or its province. Using Chengdu as an example — after entry you can explore Sichuan province, including the city center plus attractions such as Dujiangyan, Mount Emei, and Jiuzhaigou, provided your accommodation and transport remain within the province. Xi'an works similarly — the city plus the Terracotta Warriors and Mount Hua. Check the NIA website immediately before departure for the latest port status.

Thinking in terms of real itineraries: if you are flying from Europe to Japan, Shanghai is a natural transit point — Shanghai, Hangzhou, Suzhou, and Nanjing are all within 1.5 hours of each other by high-speed rail, giving you a relaxed six days across two or three cities. If you are flying from North America to Southeast Asia, Beijing is another strong option — the Beijing plus Tianjin combination covers history, food, and modern urban experiences with time to spare.

Entry and Exit Procedures

  • On arrival. Do not join the regular immigration queue for arriving passengers. Inside the arrivals hall, look for the dedicated counter marked "144-Hour Transit Without Visa" or "TWOV." Fill in the temporary entry card — passport details, flight information, and a contact address in China (the first hotel you are staying at is sufficient). Submit your passport, the card, and your third-country flight itinerary. After review, the border officer stamps a temporary entry permit into your passport with the permitted stay deadline clearly noted. Before leaving the counter, double-check the date — if there is a mistake, ask for a correction immediately. Fixing it afterward is far more difficult because the data has already been entered into the system.
  • Accommodation registration. Hotel front desks automatically scan your passport and upload the information to the public security system. If you are staying with friends, at an Airbnb, or in any non-hotel accommodation, either you or the property owner must take the passport to the local police station within 24 hours of arrival to complete a temporary residence registration form for visitors. Failing to register may lead to questioning at exit.
  • On departure. Standard procedure: check in, go through security, and proceed through the exit inspection channel. The border officer scans your passport to verify your entry record. If everything is in order, they stamp you out. Overstaying or irregular movement patterns will get you pulled aside to a separate counter — which means fines and consequences for future visits.
  • How the 144 hours are calculated. The clock starts at midnight on the day after you enter, not the moment your plane touches down. If you land at 10:30 AM on June 1, the 144-hour countdown begins at 00:00 on June 2, and your final legal departure time is 23:59 on June 7. In practice, you get close to seven full days. The same applies if you arrive at 11:00 PM — this is especially generous for red-eye flight passengers.

Common Questions and Pitfalls

  • What if I overstay or my plans change? Overstaying offers zero flexibility — the fine is 500 RMB per day, and you may be placed on an entry blacklist that affects future visa applications. A practical approach: plan four days of activities and leave two days as buffer. If you cannot leave on time due to force majeure such as illness or a flight cancellation, you must personally go to the local public security bureau's exit-entry administration department before the 144-hour deadline expires, bringing your passport and supporting documents such as the flight cancellation notice or hospital diagnosis certificate, to apply for an extension. Showing up after the deadline has passed means you are accepting a penalty, not explaining a situation.
  • Movement restrictions and itinerary tips. As long as your destination falls within the permitted zone, taking a high-speed train to another city is allowed. Beijing to Tianjin by high-speed rail — yes. Beijing to Xi'an — no. The determining factor is your disembarkation point, not the cities you pass through. A focused one-or-two-city itinerary is more than enough: six days in Beijing covers the Forbidden City, the Great Wall, and the Summer Palace; within the Shanghai-Jiangsu-Zhejiang zone, three cities are all within 1.5 hours of each other, though covering all three in six days is tight but doable. If you want to visit three or more provinces — get an L visa. The simple rule: if your destinations span two or more policy regions → L visa; if they stay within a single region → 144-hour transit.
  • When the policy does not apply, and how to choose between TWOV and an L visa. One-way tickets do not work. A return flight to your country of origin does not work. You cannot switch to another visa type after entry. You definitely cannot use it as a path to stay and work. Decision framework: one or two cities, transiting through China to a third country → 144-hour transit without visa, zero cost and zero paperwork. Three or more provinces, needing multiple entries, or planning an in-depth slow-travel trip → L visa. The deciding factor is the geographic zone, not the number of days.
  • What happens if entry is refused? If border inspection determines you do not meet the conditions, they will not issue the temporary entry permit. In this case, you will typically be directed to wait in the international transit area of the same terminal and board the next available flight to a third country. You will not be detained in a holding room, and there will be no adverse record — if you choose to apply for an L visa later, this refusal has no impact. The critical point is that your onward ticket must be in your hand. If you are refused entry and do not have a confirmed ticket out of China, the situation becomes considerably more complicated — which is why condition two (holding a third-country connecting ticket) sits at the heart of the entire policy.
  • Can I enter and exit through different ports? Yes, but they must fall within the same policy region. Shanghai entry and Hangzhou exit (Shanghai-Jiangsu-Zhejiang) — allowed. Shanghai entry and Guangzhou exit (cross-region) — not allowed. Before booking your flights, plot both cities on a map and confirm they are in the same region. Keep in mind that the port list can change — check the latest announcements before departure.
  • Pre-departure checklist. First, confirm on the NIA website that your passport nationality is on the eligible list. Second, plot your entry and exit cities on a map to verify they belong to the same policy region. Third, confirm your ticket follows the Country A → China → Third Country B pattern as a connecting itinerary. If all three checks pass, you can book with confidence — just double-check the port list for any last-minute updates before you board.

The 144-hour transit without visa policy turns a layover into a journey — zero cost, zero paperwork, processed on the spot. But the freedom comes at the price of strict conditions: your flight routing must be correct, your ports cannot span different regions, your movements must stay within the boundary, and your clock must not run out. Before departure, do three things: check the eligibility list, confirm your region, and verify your connecting itinerary. Once you land in China, follow the signs to the dedicated transit counter — and six days is yours to enjoy Peking duck in Beijing, the Bund skyline in Shanghai, or a bike ride around West Lake in Hangzhou.
