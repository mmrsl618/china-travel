A Week in Shanghai and Hangzhou

Last updated: June 2026.

Shanghai and Hangzhou sit just 180 kilometres apart — less than an hour by high-speed train — yet they feel like two different countries. Shanghai is all vertical energy: glass towers, elevated highways, the pulse of the world's busiest container port. Hangzhou is horizontal and slow: a vast lake rimmed by willow trees, hills planted with tea bushes that have been harvested for over a thousand years, temples where the air smells of incense and damp stone.

You can see both cities in a single week without rushing. This guide lays out a practical seven-day itinerary: how to split your time, which sights are worth booking in advance, what to eat in each city, where to base yourself, and how to make the train connection between them. When to Go

Spring (March to May) and autumn (September to November) are the best seasons for both cities. Temperatures sit between 15°C and 25°C, humidity is manageable, and the light is good for walking — which is how you will spend most of your time.

In spring, Hangzhou's tea fields are at their peak. The first harvest of Longjing tea happens in late March and early April, and the hills around the tea villages are vivid green. Shanghai in April and May is comfortable for long walks along the Bund and through the French Concession, though you may get occasional rain.

Autumn brings clear skies and golden light. October in Hangzhou is famous for osmanthus flowers — the whole city smells faintly sweet — but avoid the first week of October, which is Golden Week, when every major sight in both cities is packed and hotel prices can double or triple. The Labour Day holiday in early May brings a similar but shorter surge. If your dates are flexible, aim for mid-October to mid-November or late March to late April.

Summer (June to August) is hot and humid in both cities. Daytime highs regularly exceed 35°C, and July and August bring frequent thunderstorms. Winter (December to February) is cold and damp — temperatures hover around 0°C to 8°C — but crowds are thin. If you are comfortable in cold weather, January is the quietest month to visit both cities.

Days 1 to 3 — Shanghai's Must-See Sights

Three full days is enough to cover Shanghai's essentials without rushing. Here is a day-by-day plan that keeps you moving in a logical direction and avoids backtracking across the city.

Day 1: The Bund and Nanjing Road. Start on the Bund in the late afternoon — not the morning. The riverfront promenade runs for about 1.5 kilometres along the Huangpu River, and the best experience is walking it as the sun goes down. On one side, the colonial-era buildings of the 1920s and 1930s light up. On the other, the Pudong skyline — the Oriental Pearl Tower, Shanghai Tower, and Jin Mao Tower — begins to glow. The Bund is free and open at all hours. Afterwards, walk inland to Nanjing Road East, the pedestrian shopping street that stretches 5.5 kilometres. Even if you are not shopping, the neon signs and crowds are a spectacle in themselves. The pedestrian section runs from the Bund to People's Square. You can walk it in about 40 minutes at a steady pace.

Day 2: Yu Garden and Pudong. Start the morning at Yu Garden, a classical Chinese garden built in the Ming Dynasty, tucked inside Shanghai's Old City. It is compact — you need about an hour — and entry costs approximately ¥40. The surrounding Yuyuan Bazaar is touristy but worth a quick walk-through for the architecture. From there, take the metro or a taxi across the river to Pudong. Go up the Shanghai Tower observation deck on the 118th floor for the city's highest view. Tickets cost ¥180 and the deck is open from 8:30 am to 10:00 pm, with the last entry at 9:30 pm. Book ahead on Trip.com to skip the ticket queue. The view at dusk — watching the city lights come on across both sides of the river — is worth timing your visit around sunset.

Day 3: French Concession and Tianzifang. The former French Concession is Shanghai's most walkable neighbourhood: plane trees form a canopy over narrow streets, and the low-rise buildings are a mix of old lane houses and modern boutiques. Start at Fuxing Park in the morning, then wander west along Huaihai Road and south into the side streets around Wukang Road and Anfu Road. Tianzifang, a warren of alleyways converted into small shops and cafes inside traditional shikumen houses, is nearby. It gets crowded by midday, so aim to arrive by 10:00 am. Spend the rest of the afternoon exploring the area's cafes and small galleries.

Shanghai Food and Where to Stay

Shanghai's food culture is built around breakfast and snacks. The two dishes you should seek out are xiaolongbao — thin-skinned soup dumplings filled with pork and a rich broth, eaten by biting a small hole, sipping the soup, then eating the dumpling — and shengjianbao, which are thicker-skinned pan-fried pork buns with a crispy bottom and a juicy filling.

For xiaolongbao, go to Jia Jia Tang Bao on Huanghe Road, near People's Square. It is a small, utilitarian shop that has been serving some of the best soup dumplings in the city for decades. Expect to queue for 15 to 20 minutes at peak times. A basket of 12 pork xiaolongbao costs around ¥30. For shengjianbao, Yang's Dumpling on Wujiang Road is the benchmark — the buns are larger than average, with a deeply crispy base and a hot, soupy interior. A set of four costs about ¥12.

For a sit-down meal of Shanghai classics, try red-braised pork, a dish of pork belly slow-cooked in soy sauce, sugar, and spices until it is glossy and tender, and scallion oil noodles, a deceptively simple dish of thin noodles tossed in hot oil infused with scallions. Both are available at most local Shanghai-style restaurants. Lao Ji Shi on Tianjin Road and Jesse Restaurant on Tianping Road are two well-regarded mid-range options where a meal for two runs around ¥150 to ¥250.

Where to stay in Shanghai. The most convenient base is the area around People's Square or Nanjing Road East. You are within walking distance of the Bund, the metro hub of People's Square station connects three lines, and there are hotels at every budget level. Alternatively, the former French Concession — particularly around Jing'an Temple — is quieter, greener, and closer to the city's best cafes and small restaurants. The metro connects both areas to the rest of the city easily. For broader advice on booking hotels in China, see our accommodation guide.

Getting from Shanghai to Hangzhou

The Shanghai–Hangzhou high-speed rail link is one of the busiest and most convenient intercity routes in China. G-class trains depart roughly every 15 to 30 minutes throughout the day from Shanghai Hongqiao Railway Station and take between 45 minutes and 1 hour to reach Hangzhou East Station. A second-class seat costs approximately ¥73, and first class is around ¥117. Tickets go on sale 15 days before departure.

Book through the official 12306 website or use Trip.com, which has an English-language interface and accepts international payment cards. Both platforms let you select your seat preference and show real-time availability. On 12306, you will need to complete a one-time identity verification with your passport details before you can buy tickets. Trip.com handles this verification on your behalf, which is easier if you only need a few tickets.

If you are staying in central Shanghai — near People's Square or the Bund — allow about 45 minutes by metro to reach Hongqiao station. Metro Line 2 runs directly there. The station is enormous; aim to arrive at least 30 minutes before departure to clear security and find your gate. At Hangzhou East Station, Metro Line 1 connects you to the city centre and the area around West Lake in about 20 minutes. Days 4 to 7 — Hangzhou's Lakes, Temples, and Tea Villages

Hangzhou works best at a slower pace than Shanghai. The city's identity is built around West Lake, a sprawling body of water ringed by pagodas, gardens, causeways, and wooded hills. Marco Polo described it as "the finest and most splendid city in the world," and while the modern city has grown far beyond its historic core, the lake remains the centre of gravity.

Day 4: West Lake. The lake is roughly 15 kilometres in circumference. Walking the full loop takes about four to five hours, but most visitors focus on the northern and eastern shores, which contain the densest cluster of sights. Start at the Broken Bridge in the northeast corner, walk west along the Bai Causeway, and continue to the Su Causeway, a 2.8-kilometre tree-lined path that cuts across the lake from north to south. Along the way, you will pass the Autumn Moon Over the Calm Lake pavilion, the Leifeng Pagoda rising on the southern shore, and small islands visible in the distance. Entry to the lake area is free. Boat trips — available from several docks along the shore — cost around ¥55 for a standard cruise and give you a perspective you cannot get from land.

Day 5: Lingyin Temple and Feilai Peak. Lingyin is one of China's most important Buddhist temples, founded in 328 AD and still an active place of worship. The temple complex sits at the base of a forested hill, and the Feilai Peak area next to it contains hundreds of Buddhist stone carvings dating from the 10th to the 14th centuries, carved directly into the limestone cliffs. The combined ticket for the Feilai Peak scenic area and Lingyin Temple costs ¥75 total — ¥45 for the scenic area and ¥30 for the temple itself. The site opens at 7:30 am and closes at 6:00 pm. Arrive early: by 10:00 am the main halls fill with tour groups. Spend the rest of the afternoon at the quieter Yongfu Temple or Fayun Village, both within walking distance and far less crowded.

Day 6: Longjing Tea Village. Hangzhou's tea culture is inseparable from its identity. Longjing tea — translated as Dragon Well — is one of China's most famous green teas, and the terraced fields where it grows begin just a few kilometres west of the lake. The Longjing Tea Village is a cluster of traditional houses, tea shops, and fields where you can walk among the tea bushes, watch farmers at work during the spring harvest, and sit down for a tea tasting. A tasting session at a local tea house costs between ¥10 and ¥100 depending on the grade of tea you sample. There is no entry fee for the village itself. To get there, take a taxi or bus from the city centre — the drive takes about 20 minutes. Avoid the shops immediately at the village entrance that cater to large tour groups; walk deeper into the village and you will find family-run operations that are more genuine.

Day 7: Hangzhou cuisine and departure. Use your last morning to eat well. Hangzhou cuisine is one of the pillars of Zhejiang cooking: lighter than Shanghai food, with an emphasis on fresh ingredients and subtle flavours. The iconic dishes are Dongpo Pork — a square of pork belly braised until it can be cut with chopsticks, named after the Song Dynasty poet Su Dongpo — and Longjing Shrimp, river shrimp stir-fried with fresh Longjing tea leaves. Lou Wai Lou, a lakeside restaurant founded in 1848, serves both dishes with a view of West Lake. A meal for two costs around ¥200 to ¥300. For a more casual option, Hangzhou Restaurant near the lake offers the same classics at lower prices. After lunch, take Metro Line 1 back to Hangzhou East Station for your train to Shanghai or onward to your next destination.
