Essential Chinese Food Guide for First-Time Visitors

Last updated: June 2026.

Eating in China for the first time can feel overwhelming. The menus are long, the flavors are unfamiliar, and the dining customs are different from what you may be used to. But getting comfortable with Chinese food is also one of the most rewarding parts of traveling here. Meals are shared, dishes keep coming until the table is full, and the sheer variety — from delicate steamed fish to fiery Sichuan hot pot — means there is something for every palate. This guide covers what you need to know to eat well in China: how meals work, which dishes to try first, how to order without reading Chinese, and how to navigate street food and dietary restrictions with confidence.

How Chinese Meals Work

If you come from a culture where everyone orders their own plate, Chinese dining will feel like a different world. Meals here are communal. A group of people shares a collection of dishes placed in the center of the table, often on a rotating glass tray called a lazy Susan. Everyone takes small portions from the shared plates and eats them with their own bowl of rice. The logic is simple: sharing means you get to taste more things.

A typical meal in a Chinese restaurant follows a loose structure. Cold appetizers come first — sliced beef, pickled vegetables, marinated tofu. Then the hot dishes arrive, usually one after another rather than all at once. Meat, seafood, and vegetable dishes come in no fixed order, though a whole fish often appears near the end as a sign of abundance. Soup is typically served last, not first. Rice or noodles are the foundation — you order them alongside the dishes and they arrive throughout the meal. At the end, fresh fruit may appear as a complimentary dessert.

Do not be surprised if the person hosting the meal orders far more food than the group can finish. An overflowing table is a gesture of generosity and hospitality. If you are dining alone or as a couple, order two or three dishes plus rice — that is usually enough. Portions in Chinese restaurants are designed for sharing, and a single dish of mapo tofu or kung pao chicken can easily feed two people when paired with rice.

Chopsticks are the default utensil. If you are not confident with them, that is fine — most restaurants will provide a fork if you ask. But a few basic rules are worth knowing: do not stick chopsticks upright in a bowl of rice (this resembles funeral incense), do not use them to point at people, and do not hover over shared dishes with your utensils.

Must-Try Dishes for First-Timers

Chinese cuisine is enormous — eight major regional traditions and countless local specialties. For a first visitor, the sheer number of unfamiliar dishes can be paralyzing. Start with these five classics that are widely available, relatively gentle on foreign palates, and representative of major cooking styles.

Mapo Tofu (麻婆豆腐) is the most accessible introduction to Sichuan cuisine. Soft tofu cubes in a sauce of doubanjiang (fermented broad bean paste), minced pork, and Sichuan pepper. The dish is moderately spicy with a signature numbing sensation from the Sichuan peppercorns. Order it with steamed rice. If you want less heat, ask for wēilà (微辣, mild spice).

Peking Duck (北京烤鸭) is the showpiece of northern Chinese cooking. The skin is crispy and caramelized, the meat tender. It is served with thin pancakes, spring onions, and sweet bean sauce. You wrap everything yourself — take a pancake, add a slice of duck, a few onion slivers, a dab of sauce, roll it up and eat. A whole duck serves two to three people comfortably. The best places serve the skin and meat separately in courses.

Xiaolongbao (小笼包) are soup dumplings from Shanghai. Thin-skinned steamed buns filled with pork and a rich hot broth. The technique: pick one up gently with chopsticks, bite a small hole in the skin to let steam escape, slurp the soup, then eat the rest. They are served in bamboo steamers, usually eight to ten per order. Dip in black vinegar with ginger shreds.

Kung Pao Chicken (宫保鸡丁) is another Sichuan classic that has become well-known internationally. Diced chicken stir-fried with peanuts, dried chilies, and scallions in a savory-sweet sauce. The authentic version is not as sweet as Western adaptations — the dominant flavors are savory, slightly spicy, and slightly sour from Zhenjiang vinegar. It is a safe, tasty choice almost anywhere.

Hot Pot (火锅) is more an experience than a dish. A bubbling pot of broth sits in the center of the table — split into spicy half and mild half if you choose the classic yuanyang (鸳鸯) style. Raw ingredients arrive on plates: thinly sliced meat, vegetables, mushrooms, tofu, noodles. You cook them yourself in the broth. The ritual of cooking, dipping in sauce, and eating together is one of the most social dining experiences in China.

How to Order Without Reading Chinese

Not being able to read Chinese menus is one of the biggest barriers for first-time visitors. Fortunately, technology has solved this problem almost completely.

Dianping (大众点评) is the most useful app for food discovery in China. It works like Yelp combined with Instagram — browse restaurant listings with photos, ratings, and reviews. The key trick: search for a dish name, look at user-uploaded photos, and show the photo to the waiter. This bypasses the menu entirely. The app supports some English, but even the Chinese-only interface is navigable with photo browsing.

WeChat scan-and-order is now standard in most cities. Every table has a QR code. Scan it with WeChat, and a digital menu appears on your phone. Many chain restaurants and mid-range establishments have English menu options. You place the order on your phone and pay inside the app. This is the single easiest way to order food without speaking Chinese.

Point-and-smile method still works in smaller restaurants. Point at what someone else is eating and say nàge (that one). If you are in a noodle shop, point at the type of noodles you want. Restaurant staff in China are used to non-Chinese-speaking customers and will help you figure it out.

Street Food Safety and Etiquette

Street food in China is generally safe, fresh, and delicious — the high turnover means ingredients are rarely sitting out for long. The key is to use your judgment and follow local crowds. A stall with a long line of Chinese customers is a reliable indicator of quality and safety.

Popular street foods to try include jiānbǐng (煎饼, crispy crepe with egg and sauce), chuàn'r (串儿, grilled lamb skewers with cumin and chili), baozi (包子, steamed stuffed buns), and tanghulu (糖葫芦, candied hawthorn skewers). Most street food costs between 5 and 30 RMB — cheap enough to sample freely.

A few hygiene pointers: watch the stall for a minute before ordering — do they handle cash and food with the same hands? Do they change cooking oil? Is the raw food refrigerated or just sitting in the open? In practice, busy stalls in major cities are almost always fine. If you have a sensitive stomach, start with cooked-to-order items (grilled skewers, freshly fried things) rather than pre-prepared cold dishes.

Street food etiquette is simple. Eat while walking or standing near the stall. Dispose of bamboo skewers and napkins in the nearest bin. Do not haggle over street food prices — they are already at the bottom. If a stall has seating, it is fine to sit down; otherwise, consider it a standing-and-eating affair.

Dietary Restrictions — Gluten, Dairy, and Vegetarian

Navigating dietary restrictions in a Chinese food environment requires preparation. The language barrier makes it hard to communicate specifics, and Chinese cooking uses ingredients that may not be obvious to outsiders.

Gluten-free. This is the most challenging restriction in China. Soy sauce, which contains wheat, is used in nearly every savory dish. The Chinese name for gluten is miànjīn (面筋), and it appears in many vegetarian dishes as a meat substitute. Rice is naturally gluten-free, so rice-based dishes are safer choices. When ordering, say bù fàng jiàngyóu (不放酱油, no soy sauce) — but in practice, many kitchens will still use it. The safest approach is to eat at restaurants that specifically cater to gluten-free needs, which exist in major cities but are rare elsewhere. Bring a gluten-free translation card in Chinese.

Dairy-free. Traditional Chinese cooking uses very little dairy. Milk, butter, and cream are uncommon in Chinese cuisine. The main risk is in Western-style bakeries and coffee shops, and in certain dessert dishes. Hot pot broths are dairy-free. Stir-fried dishes are cooked in oil, not butter. Watch out for milk tea (奶茶, nǎichá) and certain baked goods.

Vegetarian and vegan. The concept of vegetarianism in China is different from the West. Many Chinese people who say they eat vegetarian actually eat Buddhist-style vegetarian, which excludes meat and seafood but may allow eggs and dairy. More importantly, many dishes that appear vegetarian — stir-fried greens, tofu dishes, vegetable soups — may be cooked with lard or chicken stock. When ordering, say wǒ chī sù (我吃素, I eat vegetarian) and explicitly ask fàng le ròu ma? (放了肉吗?, did you add meat?). Buddhist vegetarian restaurants (素菜馆, sùcàiguǎn) are the safest bet — they are common in cities and serve excellent mock meats made from tofu and gluten.

For all dietary restrictions, having a card or note in Chinese that clearly explains your needs is far more effective than trying to explain verbally. Show it to the waiter before ordering, and confirm with them that they understand.
