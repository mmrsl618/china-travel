China Tourist Visa (L-Visa) — A Complete Guide

Last updated: June 2026. Visa policies can change at any time. Always check the latest announcements on your local Chinese embassy or consulate website.

For anyone planning their first trip to China, the visa is often the most intimidating part. The reality is that the L-Visa process — "L" is the first letter of lüyou, the Chinese word for "travel" — is clearer than it looks. Prepare your documents carefully, submit according to the requirements, and the vast majority of applications go through without issues.

What Is the L-Visa?

The L-Visa is China's standard tourist visa, issued for sightseeing, visiting family, and private visits. It allows you to travel freely within China, but you cannot engage in any paid work — jobs, performances, interviews, and similar activities all require a different visa category.

L-Visas come in three entry types:

TypeWhat It Means
Single-entryValid for one entry. Once you leave China, the visa is used up. Best for trips where you go straight home afterward.
Double-entryAllows one exit and one re-entry. For example: Beijing → Hong Kong for a few days → back to Shenzhen. Any itinerary involving leaving and returning requires a double-entry visa.
Multiple-entryMultiple entries and exits while the visa is valid. Common options include 6-month and 1-year multiple-entry visas. Ideal if you travel to China frequently.

Most L-Visas allow a stay of 30 days. In some cases, 60 or 90 days may be granted. The exact duration depends on what the visa officer approves based on your application.

Visa validity and duration of stay are not the same thing. "Validity" means the latest date by which you must enter China. "Duration of stay" means the maximum number of days you can stay after entering. Example: your visa is valid until June 30, with a 30-day stay. You must enter by June 30 at the latest. Once inside, you can stay up to 30 days — even if those 30 days extend beyond the visa's validity date. Many travelers leave early because they confuse the two.

Who Needs an L-Visa?

First, check your passport type. China has mutual visa exemption agreements with several countries — for example, holders of ordinary passports from Singapore, Brunei, and Japan can enter visa-free for 15 to 30 days. China also offers unilateral visa-free entry to select European countries and Malaysia (China waives the visa, but the other country may not reciprocate — always verify your own re-entry requirements before departure).

If your country is on the visa-exemption list, you can skip the rest of this guide and book your flight.

If not, you'll need to meet these requirements for an L-Visa:

  1. Valid passport: Must be valid for at least 6 months beyond your planned departure date, with at least two blank visa pages.
  2. Clear itinerary: Round-trip flight tickets or a travel itinerary.
  3. Sufficient funds: You need to show you can cover your trip. Recent bank statements from the last few months are usually enough.
  4. Clean record: Previous deportation, expulsion, or entry bans may lead to rejection.

Applicants with a good travel history — especially those who have visited China before without issues — have a higher chance of getting a multiple-entry visa.

Note: Applying in your country of residence vs. a third country follows different procedures. Most people apply through the Chinese embassy, consulate, or China Visa Application Service Center (CVASC) in the country where they live. If you are abroad in a third country, you can also apply at the local Chinese embassy or consulate there, but you'll need to show proof of legal residency in that country.

Required Documents Checklist

Incomplete documents are the #1 reason applications get sent back. Go through this list item by item.

Core Documents

  • Original passport: Valid for at least 6 months beyond your departure date, with at least two blank visa pages.
  • Visa application form: Complete the online form, print it, and sign. All information must be truthful — providing false information leads to rejection and affects all future applications.
  • Recent passport photo: Color, white background. Usually 48mm × 33mm — confirm the exact spec with your local visa center.
  • Round-trip flight booking confirmation: A reservation confirmation is enough; you don't need to have paid and issued the ticket yet. Book something with free cancellation if possible.
  • Hotel reservations: Covering your entire stay. Multi-city trips require accommodation bookings for each city. If staying with a friend, they need to provide an invitation letter.
  • Travel itinerary: A brief day-by-day plan. It just needs to be reasonable, not overly detailed.

Supplementary Documents

  • Invitation letter: Issued by a Chinese citizen or legal resident in China. Should include both parties' information, relationship, purpose of visit, and who covers the costs. Having an invitation letter may simplify some requirements.
  • Bank statements: Statements from the last 3–6 months. A balance of at least 1.5× your travel budget is recommended.
  • Employment or student enrollment proof: Shows you have strong ties to your home country. Self-employed travelers can use a business license instead.
  • Old passport: If you've been to China before, the visa stamps and entry/exit records in your old passport are strong credibility markers.

Requirements vary by country and consulate. Always check the latest checklist on your local visa center's website before applying. Non-Chinese documents are technically supposed to be translated into Chinese, though in practice most visa centers accept English-language materials directly.

Application Process Step by Step

Step 1: Confirm Your Jurisdiction

Chinese embassies and consulates operate within designated jurisdictions. Find the one that covers your area of residence — you cannot apply across jurisdictions.

Step 2: Complete the Online Form

Log into the China Online Visa Application (COVA) system and fill in your details. Pay attention: your name must match your passport exactly; your employer information must match your employment letter; save the completed form as a PDF, print it, and sign. Do not falsify any information — if discovered, you'll be rejected now and all future China visa applications will be affected.

Step 3: Book an Appointment

Most visa centers require appointments. After completing the form, book a time slot on the center's website. Summer holidays and the Christmas season fill up quickly — aim to book at least a month ahead.

Step 4: Submit in Person

Go to the visa center at your scheduled time. Key points:

  • Bring all originals and photocopies.
  • Your passport will be kept during processing — usually one to two weeks. If you need your passport for other matters during that period, plan ahead.
  • Most centers collect fingerprints and take a photo on-site. Dress appropriately — the photo will appear on your visa.
  • Confirm the accepted payment methods — some centers only take cash or specific payment types. Check your local visa center's website before heading out.

Step 5: Wait for Processing

Standard processing takes 4–10 business days. Use the application reference number on your submission receipt to track progress on the visa center's website.

Step 6: Collect Your Passport

Pick it up yourself or opt for mail return. Check your visa immediately upon receiving it: name, date of birth, validity dates, number of entries, and duration of stay — any error can cause problems at the border. If you find a mistake, ask the center to correct it on the spot.

Fees and Processing Times

The total cost has two components: the visa fee (a government charge set by China, which varies by nationality and number of entries — typically between a few dozen and over a hundred US dollars) and the CVASC service fee (charged by the visa center, roughly $30–$50). The visa fee is paid at the time of submission and is non-refundable even if your application is rejected.

Multiple-entry visas cost more than single-entry, but if you think you'll visit China again, the extra cost is well worth it.

144-Hour Transit Without Visa (TWOV)

If you're only passing through China, or if you didn't have time to get a visa, the 144-hour Transit Without Visa policy — officially the "72/144-Hour Visa-Free Transit" — is an excellent alternative. With a confirmed onward ticket to a third country (or region), you can stay in designated Chinese cities visa-free — no advance application needed.

Eligibility Conditions

  1. Must be a genuine transit: Your route must be Country A → China city → Country B (or region). A → China → A does not qualify. For example, "Seoul → Beijing → Tokyo" qualifies; "Seoul → Beijing → Seoul" does not.
  2. Activity area is restricted: You may only stay within the administrative region of the entry city. If you enter in Beijing, you can only move around the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei area — you cannot travel to Shanghai.
  3. Duration: 144 hours is the theoretical maximum, calculated from midnight after the day of entry. Actual usable time depends on your flight schedule.
  4. Must hold a confirmed onward ticket: A ticket to a third country or region, confirmed and non-refundable.
  5. Limited entry ports: Major international airports in Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, Chengdu, Xi'an, Kunming, and others are generally covered. Confirm that your intended port is on the approved list before booking.

Entry Process

At the port of arrival, go to the dedicated "Transit Without Visa" lane. Present your passport and your onward ticket to the third country, fill out a temporary entry card, and you're in. Transit Without Visa cannot be extended. Overstaying leads to fines, detention, and impacts future entries.

Frequently Asked Questions

What if my application is rejected?

Common rejection reasons: incomplete or inconsistent documents, insufficient passport validity, prior adverse immigration records, inability to demonstrate ties to your home country. You can reapply after addressing the issue, though some visa centers require a waiting period before a new application. When you reapply, you must truthfully disclose the previous rejection — hiding it is worse than the rejection itself.

Can I extend my stay after entering?

Yes, but it's not easy. Apply at the local public security bureau's exit-entry administration. Extensions of up to 30 days are typical, but you'll need a valid reason and supporting documents. Approval rates for tourist visa extensions aren't high — it's better to request enough stay duration from the start.

Can I work on an L-Visa?

Absolutely not. The L-Visa explicitly prohibits any paid activities, including part-time work, teaching, translation, commercial performances, or monetizing social media content. Violations can mean fines and a deadline to leave the country, or in serious cases, detention, deportation, and being blacklisted.

Do Hong Kong and Macau count as leaving China?

Yes. Hong Kong and Macau have independent immigration systems. Traveling from mainland China to Hong Kong or Macau and back counts as exiting and re-entering. With a single-entry visa, you cannot return to the mainland after going to Hong Kong. If your itinerary includes Hong Kong or Macau, apply for at least a double-entry visa.

Are visa agencies reliable?

Reputable agencies can help organize your documents and handle the logistics, but the visa approval authority rests entirely with the Chinese embassy or consulate. Anyone claiming to "guarantee approval" or promising "special channels" is almost certainly a scam. If you choose an agency, look for one with a physical office and years of operation. Do not trust individual agents found on social media.

Most common mistakes to avoid

  • Unacceptable photo: A phone selfie is almost guaranteed to fail. Go to a professional passport photo studio.
  • Name mismatch on bookings: The name on your flight and hotel reservations must match your passport exactly.
  • Unsigned application form: After completing the form, remember to print and sign it.
  • Confusing validity with duration of stay: This is the single most common misunderstanding. Make sure you're clear before entering.

Bottom Line

The L-Visa application boils down to three things: start early, fill it out honestly, and double-check everything. Do these three and the overwhelming majority of travelers get their visa without trouble.
